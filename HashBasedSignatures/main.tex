\begin{abstract}
Asymmetric cryptography addresses critical security needs such as 
authentication, data origin verification, and key distribution. However, 
the emergence of quantum computing threatens the foundations of most 
existing asymmetric schemes. Shor's algorithm allows polynomial time 
factorization on quantum computers, jeopardizing the hardness assumptions
 of RSA and ECC. In response, the NIST launched a standardization process 
 for post-quantum cryptographic algorithms in 2016, stimulating research 
 in alternative paradigms such as lattice-based, code-based, multivariate 
 polynomial, isogeny-based, and hash-based schemes. Among them, hash-based
  signature algorithms stand out due to their conceptual simplicity and 
  reliance on well-understood primitives like hash functions. This paper 
  explores the structure and operation of hash-based signature schemes 
  including XMSS, $XMSS^{MT}$, and SPHINCS+, and discusses known 
  cryptanalytic techniques targeting them, such as intermediate 
  secret-guessing and forgery attacks.

\keywords{Post-quantum cryptography \and Hash-based signatures

\end{abstract}
